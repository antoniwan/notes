I just finished Dispatch on PC and I don't know how many hours it took me, which is actually a fine way to start a review like this because the game grabbed me hard enough that I wasn't tracking the clock, I was watching the characters and weighing the choices and scum saving once in a while just to peek at the alternate consequences before committing back to whatever my actual intent was, and that's the kind of engagement I'm looking for whenever I open a story-driven game, so I'm writing this one fresh while it's still ringing in my head.

If you read my [Crimson Desert review](/p/my-crimson-desert-review-after-200-hours/) you already know my reviewing taste comes from 30+ years of gaming across NES, SNES, all the PlayStations, PC, MMO, MOBA, RPG, indie, AAA, and the same thing applies here, because whenever I score a game that score is competing against three decades of context, and against that context Dispatch lands at an **8 out of 10**, a _"Great!"_, a **4 out of 5**, whichever scale you happen to prefer. Why not a 9? Because once you figure out the dispatch mechanic you have basically figured it out, and from that point on you are staying for the story, since the mechanics get kinda repetitive once they click and the writing has to carry the back half of the run, which is honestly a pattern I have noticed across Telltale games and the next-generation studios staffed by Telltale alumni, and AdHoc Studio (who built Dispatch) is exactly one of those, so the format ends up trading mechanical depth for narrative depth and I respect the trade, but I can't pretend the mechanics are pulling the same weight that the writing is.

## What Dispatch Is

In case you missed it, Dispatch is a narrative-driven game where you play Robert Robertson, aka MechaMan, a superhero who has moved into the dispatcher's chair. The team you're dispatching is the Z-Team, a roster of ex-supervillains trying (in their various and chaotic ways) to be on the right side of things now. You assign them to incidents based on stats, you watch how the calls play out, you make conversational and moral choices in between shifts, and the relationships you build (or neglect) shape who they become. Each shift brings the team closer together, or closer to falling apart, depending entirely on how you play. Think Invincible meets The Boys meets a tactical management layer, with way more heart than that description sounds like.

## The Dispatch Mechanic

![Dispatch screen on PC: incident details and Z-Team assignments](/images/2026/dispatch-review/dispatch-pc-game-dispatch.avif)

The gameplay loop lives on the dispatch screen, where you read an incident and match the right team member's stats and personality to the call and then live with the outcome, and once you understand how the language of the incidents maps to the stats and personalities of your team the whole thing becomes really obvious, maybe too obvious honestly, because there's a point about a third of the way in where you just stop guessing and start reading the calls the way the writers want you to read them, and after that you're not really being challenged by the mechanic anymore so much as you're using it as a tool to express whatever your values for the run happen to be.

And that's fine by me, because the dispatch screen isn't really trying to be a puzzle, it's more like a values check where every call is quietly asking you who you're willing to send into a mess, who you're willing to risk, and who you're trying to protect from the kind of incident that would break them, and once I figured out that's what the mechanic is actually for, I stopped trying to optimize it and started using it to make choices about who I wanted these people to become.

## Hacking, Honestly

The hacking minigame is straightforward. Very simple, very easy to execute. But the more time I spent inside it, the more I noticed something the developers were doing that I actually want to give them credit for. Once you get immersed in a hack, the rhythm of moving the mouse and pressing the keyboard in time with what is happening on screen starts to feel like an expression of hacking itself, like the team asked themselves how to make a player feel like they are hacking without making the player actually learn anything about hacking, and answered it with a rhythm-and-flow system that is accessible to anyone holding a mouse and a keyboard. To me that is wow, that is their way of putting hacking into a game and making it feel like the act, very cool, very creative idea, and I appreciated that the more I sat with it.

That said, it's still not deep, and I'm not going to pretend the minigame is pulling its weight on the mechanical side of this game, because underneath the clever conceptual hook it's pretty thin, so if you're coming to Dispatch for mechanical depth then hacking is not where it lives, it's a beat between story scenes, and I'd honestly rather have a thin minigame that doesn't break the pacing than a deep one that keeps yanking me out of the story I actually came here for.

## The Real Game Is the Writing

Look, the story and the choices and the characters and the worldbuilding, that's the actual game, that's why I was engaged and that's why I was immersed, because if you stripped the dispatch mechanic out entirely you would still have a better superhero story than 90% of the superhero content being shipped right now in any medium.

The characters are doing all the work, and they're doing it well, because the Z-Team is full of people you start caring about whether you wanted to or not, some of them funny in the Invincible way where there's casual brutality wrapped in charm and some of them tragic in the Boys way where the costume is just a layer over a person who got broken at some point, and the dialogue is sharp, the voice acting is committed, and the writers were clearly setting up payoffs two or three scenes ahead because I kept noticing little beats land that I hadn't realized were being planted.

The worldbuilding is the part I keep coming back to days after the credits, because every faction and every offhand reference to the wider hero industry and every news clip and every random comment about somebody else's career stacks up into this sense that there's a whole superhero economy running outside the dispatch room, you can feel the weight of it pressing on your desk, and the game pulls it off without ever dumping lore on you, which is harder than it sounds because most games can't help themselves.

## The MechaMan Arc Is the Heart of It

If you ask me which incidents stood out and which beats I'll actually remember, it's the MechaMan arc, because that's the spine of the whole game for me and probably the part I'll still be thinking about a year from now.

MechaMan starts the game locked into what I'd call extreme focus, the cape is the identity and the mission is the identity and there's basically no separation between him and the work, no oxygen for anything else, and then over the course of the game he comes out of that, he starts actually living with the Z-Team instead of just leading them, he works, he has relationships, he makes mistakes that aren't tactical mistakes but human ones, and the game lets him sit inside those mistakes long enough for them to matter, so he ends up present and involved and around and imperfect about all of it, because that's what actually happens when somebody loosens their grip on the mission.

And that's the version of being a hero the writers are arguing for, and I'm with them on it, because heroism isn't really about the kill or the power level or the lonely focus that grinds your humanity into nothing, it's about helping people and staying in relationship with the ones standing next to you and doing the right thing when the right thing is small and inconvenient and not the cinematic option, so watching MechaMan figure that out while I was sitting at the dispatch screen trying to figure out my own version of it in real time was the most resonant part of the whole run.

I won't pretend I didn't see myself in that arc, because the version of a man who can't separate himself from the work is a version I know pretty well, and the slow work of coming back to the table and the relationships and the mistakes and the present moment is work I recognize from my own life, so the fact that this game managed to put that question in front of me through a dude in a mech suit is honestly a credit to whoever wrote him.

## The Choices and Scum Saving

I'll admit it: I scum saved a few times. Not because I was trying to break the game or chase the optimal ending, but because every now and then I'd pick what I thought was the obvious choice and the result would be the exact opposite of what I was going for. So I'd reload and see if I was reading the prompt wrong, or if the writers were going somewhere I hadn't tracked. Once I understood what I had actually been pointing at, I'd commit to it. I don't know if that counts as cheating. I'm calling it intent alignment. The choices in this game are real and the consequences branch in ways I wasn't expecting, so I'd rather take the extra two minutes to make sure my action matches my values than walk away from a moment going "wait, that's not what I meant."

For the actual run I stuck true to my values, played the dispatcher I would want to be, compassionate when compassion seemed earned and firm when firmness was clearly needed and patient with the people on the team who were still figuring themselves out, because that's basically how I try to move through my real life too and it didn't make any sense to suddenly become a different person inside the game.

I played the whole thing solo, but Zuleyka walked by once during a romantic cutscene and went "whoa, romantic," and honestly that was the extent of audience participation in this run, which ended up being a pretty good signal of the writing quality, because a non-gamer passing through the room generally only stops when something on screen is actually working.

## The Ending I Got (Spoilers Ahead)

I got what I'm calling the deadly ending, where Invisigal (or Invisibitch, depending on which side of her she's leaning into that day) embraced her full villainy and the cost of that turn got paid in blood and consequence across the rest of the Z-Team, and it was expected and unexpected at the same time, expected because I'd been pouring most of my energy into Blonde Blazer for the whole run (she's the main support character, secondary lead, my boss in the dispatch room, a superhero in her own right, and she got pretty much all of my dispatches and conversation time and relationship beats), and unexpected because Invisigal was getting some attention from me too, just not the kind of sustained, consistent anchoring she actually needed, and so she eventually slipped back into the person she'd been before because nobody on the dispatch side was paying close enough attention to keep her pointed at the person she was trying to become.

And honestly that's what makes the ending land, because it doesn't feel arbitrary or punitive, it feels earned by whatever version of myself the game had been quietly watching me become, so I didn't get a "bad" ending because the writers had decided to punish me, I got the ending I actually built with my attention, and some of those choices were obvious branches with obvious signposts but a lot of them were quiet attention budgets I didn't even know I was spending in real time.

It also lands harder when you remember the Z-Team are ex-supervillains, because Invisigal turning full villain isn't really an unprovoked plot twist, it's a backslide, and the whole premise of dispatching this team is that they're trying not to be who they used to be, which means the dispatcher is one of the people responsible for keeping that effort alive, and I let one of them slip, so that one's on me.

If I replay (and I might), I want to see what happens when Invisigal is the one I anchor and Blonde Blazer is the one I let drift, because I'm honestly curious what the mirror version of my run looks like, and the fact that I'm even considering it tells you the replay value is real, which is something most narrative games can't actually pull off.

## The Hero Genre Context

Here's the part of the review that's going to stick with me long after I've stopped thinking about the mechanics, because from now on every time I watch a hero show I'm going to be coming back to Dispatch, and when I say every hero show I mean Invincible (where I'll be thinking about how Dispatch handled the weight of being a hero inside a world where heroes are also a workforce), and I mean The Boys (where I'll be thinking about how Dispatch handled the gap between the cape and the person inside it without going as cynical as The Boys does), and I mean literally any cartoon or anime or superhero movie that touches the question of what it actually means to be a hero or what it means to lead a team of them, because the writers of this game nailed that question, whoever was leading this story clearly understood heroes and hero groups and the daily ethical work of being a hero in general, and they translated all of that understanding into a game.

That's high praise coming from me, because I came into Dispatch expecting a fun narrative game and I left with a new reference point for an entire genre I've been consuming for decades.

## The Art and the Polish

I almost forgot to say this earlier, but the game is also genuinely beautiful, because the art direction is gorgeous and the stylized comic-book sensibility makes every frame feel like a panel pulled out of an animated series, the character designs are distinct enough that I never had to wonder who I was looking at even in the busiest ensemble shots, and the cutscenes have an animation quality and a sound design that actually carry the emotional weight of whatever scene they're sitting inside. Remember when Zuleyka walked by during that romantic cutscene and went "whoa, romantic"? That wasn't just the writing landing, that was the whole scene built well enough that a non-gamer registered the craft from across the room, and honestly that's a pretty serious benchmark for any cutscene in any medium to hit.

The polish runs deep too, and as somebody who builds software for a living I notice the quiet stuff, the loading transitions that don't break immersion, the menus that feel like part of the world, the absence of obvious bugs or rendering hiccups across my whole playthrough, the audio mixing properly between dialogue and ambient and score. The code under this thing is doing its job, and the team clearly cared about every layer, which is the kind of craft that used to be standard at the top of the industry and is now actually rare. Big credit to AdHoc for shipping a game that looks and sounds and feels as good as the writing reads.

## Should You Play It

Yes, you should play it, especially if you care about story and character, and especially if you've ever enjoyed Invincible or The Boys or any of the deconstruct-the-superhero stuff in any other medium, and especially if you like narrative games where the choices actually matter and the writing earns its emotional beats instead of begging for them.

Maybe not, if you're coming in for mechanical depth in the dispatch loop or the hacking, or if you're coming in expecting the kind of role-play freedom you get from a character builder, because the dispatch mechanic is satisfying but it isn't a brain teaser, the hacking is filler with a clever idea on top of it, and the whole game is honestly a story machine wearing a management game's clothes, which is exactly what I wanted but might not be what you want.

One more heads up specifically for the superhero game crowd, especially anyone who cut their teeth on City of Heroes or any of the build-your-own-cape RPGs: Dispatch is not that, there is no character customization, no costume designing, and the limited power-picking the game does offer mostly feeds into the dispatch sections of the story rather than into the narrative itself, so you play MechaMan and the role-play lives entirely inside his established character and the Z-Team's already-written personalities, which is focused or limited depending on what you came for. I think the focus is actually one of the reasons the writing lands so hard, because a known protagonist lets the writers commit to a real arc instead of leaving room for a thousand possible custom builds, but the trade is real and worth knowing about going in.

**8 out of 10. 4 out of 5.** The writing and the cast and the art direction and the cutscenes and the sound and the polish and the thesis on heroism are all firing at a level that puts this game on my reference shelf for the genre going forward, and the two points I'm holding back are for repetitiveness and lack of mechanic depth, full stop, because the dispatch loop reveals itself early and the hacking never really grows past its clever idea, so after that point you are staying for the story (which, to be fair, ended up being more than enough for me). Worth your time and worth your attention, and big thanks to the AdHoc team for proving the Telltale DNA still has plenty of life in it.
